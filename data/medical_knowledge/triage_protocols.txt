MEDICAL KNOWLEDGE BASE - TRIAGE PROTOCOLS AND RISK ASSESSMENT
Source: Emergency Triage Guidelines (Educational Reference)

=============================================================
TRIAGE RISK LEVELS
=============================================================

RISK LEVEL: LOW (Green)
- Characteristics: Stable vitals, non-life-threatening, no urgent intervention needed
- Examples: Common cold, mild headache, mild sprain, mild gastroenteritis, minor skin rash
- Action: Self-care, monitor symptoms, see GP if persists
- Timeframe: Can wait 24-48 hours or schedule routine appointment

RISK LEVEL: MODERATE (Yellow / Orange)
- Characteristics: Uncomfortable/significant symptoms but not immediately life-threatening; may deteriorate
- Examples: Moderate fever, significant pain, persistent vomiting, moderately controlled chronic disease flare
- Action: Seek medical attention within hours to 1 day; urgent care or GP same day
- Timeframe: Evaluation needed within 4-24 hours

RISK LEVEL: HIGH (Red)
- Characteristics: Life-threatening potential, time-sensitive, requires immediate medical attention
- Examples: Chest pain, stroke symptoms, severe allergic reaction, high fever with neck stiffness, severe abdominal pain, loss of consciousness
- Action: CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES (911/112/999) or go to Emergency Department IMMEDIATELY
- Timeframe: Minutes matter

=============================================================
RED FLAG SYMPTOMS (Always HIGH Risk - Seek Emergency Care)
=============================================================

CARDIOVASCULAR RED FLAGS:
- Chest pain, pressure, tightness, or squeezing (especially with radiation to arm, jaw, back)
- Sudden severe shortness of breath
- Palpitations with dizziness or near-syncope
- Sudden leg swelling with chest pain (possible DVT/PE)

NEUROLOGICAL RED FLAGS:
- Sudden severe "thunderclap" headache (worst headache of life)
- Sudden weakness or numbness on one side of body
- Sudden speech difficulty or confusion
- Sudden vision loss or double vision
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures (new onset)

RESPIRATORY RED FLAGS:
- Severe shortness of breath at rest
- Lips or fingertips turning blue (cyanosis)
- Inability to speak full sentences due to breathlessness
- Stridor (high-pitched breathing sound)

GASTROINTESTINAL RED FLAGS:
- Vomiting blood (hematemesis) or coffee-ground emesis
- Passing black tarry stools (melena)
- Rigid, board-like abdomen
- Severe abdominal pain with syncope

INFECTION RED FLAGS (Sepsis Criteria):
- High fever (>39°C) OR hypothermia (<36°C) with:
  * Rapid heart rate (>90 bpm)
  * Rapid breathing (>20 breaths/min)
  * Altered mental status
  * Known or suspected infection source

PEDIATRIC RED FLAGS (Children):
- High fever in infants <3 months (any fever)
- Bulging fontanelle
- Non-blanching rash with fever (possible meningococcal)
- Severe lethargy or difficult to rouse
- Severe dehydration signs

=============================================================
VITAL SIGNS REFERENCE (Adults)
=============================================================

NORMAL RANGES:
- Temperature: 36.1–37.2°C (97–99°F)
- Heart Rate: 60–100 bpm
- Blood Pressure: <120/80 mmHg (normal), 120-129/<80 (elevated), ≥130/80 (hypertension)
- Respiratory Rate: 12–20 breaths/minute
- Oxygen Saturation (SpO2): 95–100%
- Blood Glucose (fasting): 70–100 mg/dL (3.9–5.6 mmol/L)

CONCERNING VALUES:
- Fever: >38°C (100.4°F) — High fever: >39.5°C
- Tachycardia: >100 bpm
- Bradycardia: <50 bpm
- Hypotension: Systolic <90 mmHg or drop >20 mmHg from baseline
- Tachypnea: >24 breaths/minute
- Low SpO2: <95% (concerning), <90% (emergency)

=============================================================
PAIN SCALE REFERENCE
=============================================================

Numeric Pain Scale (0-10):
- 0-3: Mild pain — Low risk indicator
- 4-6: Moderate pain — Moderate risk indicator  
- 7-10: Severe pain — High risk indicator (especially if sudden onset)

Pain Character Assessment:
- Burning: Consider GERD, UTI, neuropathic pain
- Crushing/Squeezing: Consider cardiac origin — HIGH RISK
- Stabbing: Can be pleuritic, gastrointestinal, or musculoskeletal
- Throbbing: Often vascular; consider migraine, dental, abscess
- Colicky (comes and goes in waves): Consider renal/biliary colic, intestinal obstruction
- Constant/progressive: More concerning than intermittent

=============================================================
RISK STRATIFICATION RULES
=============================================================

AUTOMATIC HIGH RISK if any of these are present:
1. Chest pain or pressure
2. Difficulty breathing at rest
3. Stroke symptoms (FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty)
4. Altered consciousness or confusion
5. Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis): throat swelling, hives with breathing difficulty
6. Suspected sepsis
7. Suicidal ideation
8. Suspected poisoning or overdose
9. Major trauma
10. Non-blanching rash with fever

ELEVATED TO MODERATE if:
1. Fever >38.5°C lasting >48 hours
2. Severe pain (7+/10)
3. Vomiting or diarrhea >24 hours with dehydration signs
4. Known chronic disease in poor control (uncontrolled diabetes, asthma, etc.)
5. Symptoms worsening despite self-treatment
6. Age >65 or <2 years with significant illness
7. Immunocompromised with fever
8. Pregnancy with concerning symptoms

STAYS LOW if:
1. Mild symptoms (<5/10 pain)
2. No systemic features (no fever, no altered vital signs implied)
3. Symptoms <24-48 hours in otherwise healthy adult
4. Clear benign etiology (e.g., caught a cold after known exposure)
5. No red flag symptoms present
6. Improving trend

=============================================================
COMMON DRUG INTERACTIONS AND CONTRAINDICATIONS (Reference)
=============================================================

NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen):
- Avoid in: Renal impairment, peptic ulcer disease, heart failure, late pregnancy
- Caution with: Anticoagulants (increased bleeding risk), ACE inhibitors, diuretics

Paracetamol/Acetaminophen:
- Avoid: Liver disease, excessive alcohol use
- Max dose: 4g/day in adults; 2g/day in liver disease

Aspirin:
- Avoid in: Children <16 (Reye's syndrome), peptic ulcer, anticoagulant therapy
- Use with caution: Elderly, renal impairment

Antihistamines:
- Cause drowsiness (first-generation): caution driving/operating machinery
- Avoid: Urinary retention, glaucoma (anticholinergic effects)

=============================================================
WHEN TO SEEK EMERGENCY CARE - PATIENT GUIDANCE
=============================================================

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES (911/112) IMMEDIATELY IF:
✓ Chest pain or pressure lasting >15 minutes
✓ Difficulty breathing
✓ Sudden weakness or numbness on one side
✓ Sudden severe headache unlike any before
✓ Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
✓ Suspected stroke (FAST positive)
✓ Severe allergic reaction with breathing difficulty
✓ Suspected heart attack
✓ Seizure lasting >5 minutes or first-time seizure

GO TO EMERGENCY DEPARTMENT URGENTLY IF:
✓ High fever with stiff neck and photophobia
✓ Moderate chest pain
✓ Severe abdominal pain
✓ Signs of infection with systemic symptoms
✓ Serious injury with potential fracture
✓ Mental health crisis with safety concerns

PREVENTIVE HEALTH GUIDELINES
=============================================================

CARDIOVASCULAR PREVENTION:
- Regular blood pressure monitoring
- Cholesterol screening every 5 years (more frequently if elevated)
- Maintain healthy weight (BMI 18.5-24.9)
- Exercise: 150 minutes moderate-intensity per week
- Smoking cessation
- Limit alcohol intake

DIABETES PREVENTION:
- Maintain healthy weight
- Regular physical activity
- Balanced diet low in refined carbohydrates and saturated fats
- Blood glucose screening if risk factors present

CANCER SCREENING (General):
- Cervical: Pap smear every 3 years (age 21-65)
- Breast: Mammogram every 2 years (age 40-74, discuss with doctor)
- Colorectal: Colonoscopy starting age 45, or earlier with family history
- Lung: Low-dose CT if heavy smoker age 50-80

VACCINATION SCHEDULE (Adults):
- Annual influenza vaccine
- COVID-19 as per current guidelines
- Pneumococcal (age >65 or high-risk)
- Tdap (tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis) every 10 years
- Shingles (zoster) vaccine age >50
